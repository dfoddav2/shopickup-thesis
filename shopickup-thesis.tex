% Abstract (English)
\begin{abstract}
  E-commerce merchants, especially SMEs, often face fragmented shipping workflows due to heterogeneous carrier APIs and limited end-to-end visibility into shipping performance.
  This bachelor thesis presents \textit{Shopickup}, a multi-carrier shipping integration and monitoring platform that addresses this problem through two contributions.
  First, drawing on service-oriented architecture principles, the Adapter pattern, and empirical evidence of REST API heterogeneity, an extensible carrier-adapter core combined with an API-gateway front door is proposed and implemented as an open-source integration layer, currently supporting three Hungarian carriers (MPL, GLS, Foxpost).
  Comparative analysis against four alternative architectural styles confirms this combination as the most suitable trade-off between extensibility and operational complexity for SME-scale systems.
  Second, a KPI-driven analytics dashboard is designed around a Lambda-inspired hybrid Speed/Batch/Serving Layer architecture, balancing near-real-time operational alerts (e.g., SLA breaches) with historically comprehensive strategic visualizations (e.g., carrier cost comparisons, delivery-cycle trends).
  The system was evaluated through semi-structured interviews with two SME merchants following a two-week trial period.
  Findings confirm that the hybrid layered design successfully supports both operational and strategic decision-making without requiring merchants to reason about underlying data freshness, and identify On Time Delivery Rate, SLA compliance, per-carrier delivery times, and cost comparisons as the most consistently valued KPIs.
  The interviews also surfaced an unanticipated KPI gap around Cash-on-Delivery and pickup-point non-collection, not addressed in the reviewed literature, suggesting a promising direction for future research specific to markets with high automated parcel machine adoption.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

E-commerce has revolutionized the retail landscape, enabling businesses of all sizes to reach domestic and global markets with unprecedented ease, meanwhile consumers benefit from unparalleled convenience and choice.
Consequently, online shopping has seen strong growth over the past two decades, a trend accelerated by technological advancements and shifting consumer behaviors. As per Capital One's 2025 report, online sales accounted for 20.5\% of total retail sales worldwide \cite{capitaloneECommerceStatistics20252025} and has been steadily climbing over the past decade.
Similar trends can be observed in various regional markets and their respective research reports, such as in Eurostat's EEA specific data  showing that more and more stores are adopting online sales channels, with 23.83\% of all enterprises having one in 2023, compared to just 17.21\% ten years prior \cite{eurostatEcommerceStatistics2025}, with the share of total sales following suit.
On the individual level, Eurostat surveyed that 94\% of EU citizens used the internet in 2024 and 77\% have made online purchases in the past year \cite{eurostatEcommerceStatisticsIndividuals2025}, with younger demographics leading the way, showing that this growth is likely to continue in the foreseeable future.

We have witnessed the rise of giants like Amazon and the Alibaba group, who have set new standards for customer experience, logistics, and supply chain management on a global scale. These large enterprises have proven that efficient e-commerce operations can drive significant revenue growth and to this day in Europe, larger enterprises are more likely to sell online than smaller ones, with 24.44\% of turnover coming from online sales in large companies, compared to just 14.99\% for medium and 9.49\% for small enterprises \cite{eurostatEcommerceStatistics2025}.
However, the e-commerce boom is not limited to large players, small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) have more opportunities and tools under their disposal than ever before to establish and grow their online presence.
Webshop engines like Shopify, Wix and Wordpress based solutions like WooCommerce have democratized access to e-commerce, allowing SMEs to set up online stores with relative ease and minimal upfront investment.
This lowers the barrier to entry for many small businesses and aspiring entrepreneurs, leading to a remarkable number of 24.1 million online stores globally \cite{capitaloneHowManyOnline2025}.
We can also see that most of the online stores are created with a just a handful of these popular platforms, with Shopify alone powering 23.6\% of them \cite{capitaloneHowManyOnline2025}.
These giant platforms can not by themselves cover all the needs of the diverse e-commerce landscape across different regions, industries and business models, thus they need some help from third-party applications and services to fill in the gaps.
This thesis project, commonly referred to as \textit{Shopickup} for the practical implementation, comes into play, to solve a major pain point for SMEs in the e-commerce space: the integration and management of shipping carriers.

\section{Motivation and Problem Statement}
While e-commerce platforms provide solid foundations for online sales, due to their general-purpose large-scale international nature, they can not possibly cover all shipping providers of all regions they operate in.
SMEs similarly often struggle with fragmented shipping workflows, where order data must be manually transferred between sales platforms like Shopify and heterogeneous carrier systems like GLS, DPD, DHL and many others.
This fragmentation leads to operational inefficiencies, data redundancy, and a lack of real-time visibility into shipping performance.
To address these challenges, webshop engines allow third-party applications to extend their core functionalities via well-defined APIs.

Multiple such third-party applications already exist, but they most often have limited carrier or region support, or are built for specific use-cases, such as label generation only.
They typically rely on platform-specific plugins that are difficult to maintain or monolithic middleware that lacks the flexibility to adapt to new carrier standards rapidly.
This project addresses the lack of a unified, extensible integration layer that can:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., left=0pt, itemsep=3pt]
  \item Normalize data across diverse carrier Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) without completely custom code for each new provider.
  \item Provide a scalable analytics framework that transforms raw shipping data into actionable business intelligence.
\end{enumerate}

There is a clear need for a more comprehensive, flexible, and scalable solution that can integrate multiple carriers, is easily extensible to support new ones, and provides meaningful insights into shipping performance via key performance indicators (KPIs).
The motivation for this project also stems from practical experience supporting a Shopify-based online store.

\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related}
Finding existing literature that directly addresses the design of an extensible, multi-carrier shipping integration layer for SMEs is challenging. Most scientific work either focuses on logistics and supply chains (routing, last-mile optimization, warehouse management) or on general software architecture and integration patterns (SOA, microservices, API gateways). There is a clear gap when it comes to shipping integrations at the API level between e-commerce platforms and parcel carriers.
This chapter therefore proceeds in two steps. First, it reviews general software architecture approaches for integrating heterogeneous APIs and argues how these can be applied to the domain of multi-carrier shipping. Second, it briefly positions existing commercial and open-source solutions relative to the architectural patterns discussed, thereby highlighting the research gap addressed by this thesis.

\section{Software Architecture for API Integration}

The core technical problem of this thesis is the integration of multiple heterogeneous carrier APIs into a single, unified application in a way that remains extensible when adding new carriers or changing existing ones.
This problem is a specific instance of the broader topic of enterprise application integration and service-oriented architecture (SOA).

\subsection{Service-Oriented Architecture as Theoretical Foundation}

Let us first establish a theoretical foundation based on SOA principles. What better way to start than with a definition?
While there is evidently no single agreed-upon definition of SOA \cite{niknejadUnderstandingServiceOrientedArchitecture2020}, for the purpose of introducing it we turn to the book of Juric et al., which provides a concise and practical definition:

\begin{displayquote}
  "In short, it is an Enterprise Architecture where applications are designed to provide coarse-grained services, which are consumed by Business Processes or other integration applications.
  Service-Oriented Architecture is both a design concept and an architecture.
  The design concept in SOA is about designing applications/systems that have well-defined self-describing access interfaces, with the services being composed into business processes.
  The architecture is about having simple mechanisms to use these access-interfaces for Integration of the Enterprise." \cite{juricSOAApproachIntegration2007}
\end{displayquote}

It is easy to see how this thesis's projects calls for use of SOA practices, specifically the architectural approach of simple mechanisms for using different access-interfaces.
Papazoglou emphasizes that it “captures the logical way of designing a software system to provide services to end-user applications or other services distributed over a network” \cite{papazoglouServiceOrientedArchitectures2007}. 
The key principle is loose coupling, achieved by a clear separation between service interfaces and service implementations.
In a SOA-based design, an application does not directly depend on the internal details of another application, instead it interacts with well-defined service contracts.
For the problem at hand, this translates to the idea that our core system should not be tightly coupled to the internal details of DHL's, GLS's, or Austrian Post's APIs, as these are likely to change over time and differ significantly between providers anyway.
Instead, it should rely on a stable, domain-specific interface (e.g., “create shipment,” “track shipment,” “cancel shipment”) outlining the capabilities required for users of our system, while carrier-specific logic is encapsulated in separate components behind these interfaces and abstractions.

A systematic literature review by Niknejad et al. synthesizes 103 primary studies and concludes that much of the case studies and implementations of SOA show the positive impact of the before mentioned characteristics and that it has "proven to be a key paradigm in numerous industries" \cite{niknejadUnderstandingServiceOrientedArchitecture2020}. 
It also highlighted however, that the biggest problem with SOA adoption is the topic of governance and the lack of empirical studies on measuring its success, however these are out of scope for this thesis.
Applying these principles to this project, we can outline the following high-level architectural guidelines:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item The core business logic (e.g., order management, user interface) interacts with a generic shipping service interface rather than individual carrier APIs.
  \item Each carrier integration is treated as a service that implements this generic interface.
  \item Adding or replacing a carrier should require only changes in the implementation of the corresponding service, not in the rest of the system.
\end{itemize}

This conceptual foundation is important, but it does not yet specify concrete implementation structures or design patterns.

\subsection{Empirical Evidence of REST API Heterogeneity}

In practice, most modern integrations are realized via RESTful web APIs over HTTP.
A common assumption in industry is that REST APIs are similar, implying that integration should be straightforward across different providers.
However, this is not necessarily the truth in most cases.
Neumann et al. concluded through an extensive empirical study of 522 public web service APIs that there is “high diversity in services, including differences in adherence to best practices, with only 0.8\% of services strictly complying with all REST principles.” \cite{neumannAnalysisPublicREST2021} and that even among “REST” APIs, there is only limited compliance to strict REST constraints.
In other words, there is no real homogeneity at the API level as each provider does things slightly differently.
One very common example many developers encounter day by day is the variety of authentication methods or the lack of a variety of available REST methods (GET, POST, PUT, DELETE, etc.) for operations that would logically map to them.

Translating this to the parcel-shipping context, even if DHL, GLS, Austrian Post, and others expose “REST” APIs, their URL structures, authentication, request bodies, responses, and error handling differ in non-trivial ways.
In the context of shipping provider APIs there exist multiple levels of format heterogeneity, from the response itself varying between JSON and XML for legacy developers, to the label format of PDF or ZPL.
A naive point-to-point design that integrates each carrier directly into the core application will inevitably accumulate a large amount of carrier-specific branching logic, making the system brittle and hard to maintain.
The empirical heterogeneity demonstrated by Neumann et al. thus again provides a strong justification for an explicit abstraction and normalization layer aligning with SOA guidelines, rather than relying on ad-hoc HTTP calls scattered throughout the codebase.

\subsection{Adapter Pattern for Carrier Abstraction}

The challenge of integrating heterogeneous systems with incompatible interfaces is not new to software engineering.
The Adapter pattern is a well-established structural design pattern introduced in the foundational work “Design Patterns: Elements of Reusable Object-Oriented Software” by Gamma et al. all the way back in 1994 \cite{gammaDesignPatternsElements1994}. 
It works by introducing an intermediary adapter that implements a target interface (the one the client expects) while internally delegating to an adaptee (the existing component with an incompatible interface).

This pattern has two primary implementation variants, one commonly referred to as Class Adapter, which is inheritance based, and the other as Object Adapter, which is composition based.
In the context of this project, the latter one is more appropriate, as we are dealing with external carrier APIs with no benefit of inheritance.
The following is a simplified illustration of how the Adapter pattern can be applied to the multi-carrier shipping integration problem:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,caption={Carrier adapter interface pseudocode},label=lst:carrier-adapter-interface]
interface CarrierAdapter {
    createLabel(order): Label
    trackShipment(trackingId): TrackingInfo
    cancelShipment(trackingId): CancellationResult
  }
\end{lstlisting}

For each carrier, a dedicated adapter implements this interface, endowing it with the abilities of the domain model while encapsulating the carrier-specific details:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,caption={Carrier adapter implementation pseudocode},label=lst:carrier-adapter-implementations]
class DHLAdapter implements CarrierAdapter { ... }
class GLSAdapter implements CarrierAdapter { ... }
class AustrianPostAdapter implements CarrierAdapter { ... }
\end{lstlisting}

Each adapter is responsible for:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item Translating the generic \textit{Shopickup} domain model into the carrier-specific request model (e.g., mapping address fields, parcel dimensions, and service options).
  \item Calling the carrier's API, including managing authentication, endpoints, and error conditions.
  \item Translating the carrier's response into a normalized internal representation suitable for downstream processing and analytics.
\end{itemize}

This pattern has several benefits, which align closely with the quality attributes targeted by this thesis:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item Encapsulation of change: When a carrier changes their API (e.g., URL, auth method, required fields), only the corresponding adapter needs modification, not the rest of the system.
  \item Extensibility: Adding a new carrier requires implementing a new `CarrierAdapter` without altering existing adapters or the core logic, directly supporting the Open/Closed Principle.
  \item Testability: Each adapter can be unit-tested in isolation with mock carrier APIs.
\end{itemize}

The Adapter pattern therefore provides a concrete, implementation-level mechanism to realize the carrier-agnostic abstraction suggested by SOA and motivated by REST API heterogeneity.

\subsection{API Gateway as a Unified Front Door}

While the Adapter pattern addresses the structural coupling problem at the code level, it does not by itself solve several cross-cutting concerns that inevitably arise in a multi-carrier context:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item Authentication and authorization of external clients
  \item Rate limiting and throttling (e.g., to respect carrier rate limits)
  \item Central logging and monitoring of requests
  \item Versioning and gradual rollout of new functionality
  \item Caching of frequently requested data (e.g., tracking information)
\end{itemize}

In microservice and distributed architectures, these concerns are commonly handled using an API Gateway as an extra intermediary layer.
Ochuba et al. have written an extensive review on API Gateway patterns \cite{ochubaSystematicReviewAPI2021} where they first argue that with the rise of SOA and microservices, API Gateways have become a foundational component in modern scalable, secure systems, particularly when integrating multiple backend services.

\begin{displayquote}{}
  "As enterprises increasingly adopt service-oriented designs, the need for robust intermediary layers that can handle diverse client protocols and varied backend requirements has become essential.
  In this context, API gateways not only provide a unified interface but also act as enforcement points for both technical and business logic" \parencite{ochubaSystematicReviewAPI2021}
\end{displayquote}

They also identified a number of roles and patterns an API Gateway typically plays such as Aggregator, Proxy, Adapter for roles and patterns such as Edge Caching, which could be very relevant for some functionalities of our use case \cite{ochubaSystematicReviewAPI2021}. 
Applied to \textit{Shopickup}, an API Gateway would serve the following purposes:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item Expose a single REST API towards the admin client with stable, per capability endpoints like `POST /create-label` and `GET /shipments/{id}/tracking`.
  \item Internally, route requests to the appropriate carrier adapter service based on parameters such as the selected carrier and configured routing rules.
  \item Provide centralized rate limiting per carrier if required.
  \item Ensure consistent logging and monitoring across all carrier integrations.
\end{itemize}

By combining the Adapter pattern at the service level with an API Gateway at the edge, the project could offer a single, coherent interface to clients while encapsulating all carrier-specific complexity in backend modules.
This architectural combination directly supports the goal of building an extensible, maintainable multi-carrier integration layer.

\subsection{Extensibility as a Measurable Architectural Quality}

Extensibility has been mentioned multiple times as a key design goal for this thesis, making it a crucial aspect to evaluate. It is not only a design intention but can actually be treated as a measurable quality attribute.
The technical report by the Software Engineering Institute (SEI) at CMU discusses extensibility as a specific architectural concern and outlines how extension points and extension strategies should be identified and evaluated \cite{kazmanExtensibility2022}. 
In the following table I will present their view on how a system's extensibility can be assessed, and how these criteria map to the thesis domain of multi-carrier shipping integration.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{@{}>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{6cm}p{9cm}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Criterion}                                          & \textbf{Thesis domain example}                                                                            \\
    \midrule
    Appropriateness of extension points                         &
    The \texttt{CarrierAdapter} interface (create/track/cancel) and its method signatures cover the majority of required carrier behaviors and potential extension options. \\[6pt]
    Degree of encapsulation and coupling                        &
    Can adapters be added/removed without touching core business logic? Evaluates whether carrier-specific code is fully localized to adapter modules.                      \\[6pt]
    Cohesion of extension mechanisms                            &
    Adapter implementations contain only carrier-specific responsibilities (mapping, auth, error handling) and avoid leaking cross-cutting concerns.                        \\[6pt]
    Extension lifecycle: performance, testing, deployment, etc. &
    Ease of unit/integration testing of adapters in isolation; expected performance impact; deployment/update workflow for individual adapters.                             \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Extensibility evaluation criteria as defined by Kazman et al. \cite{kazmanExtensibility2022} and how they map to the thesis domain}
\end{table}

In parallel, Petrillo et al. have done a literature review of 56 studies, showing which metrics are most commonly used by researchers.
They found that coupling was one of the most frequently measured metrics and highlighted that generally low coupling and high cohesion are essential for extensible architectures \cite{coulinSoftwareArchitectureMetrics2019}. 
Moreover, of these 56 papers, 6 had extensibility as their main focus, showing that it is an actively researched topic, especially with the rise of agile development practices and SOAs.
These theoretical and metric-based perspectives support using extensibility, coupling, and cohesion as criteria when comparing different architectural options and could be used later in the evaluation chapter to assess the chosen design.

\section{Architectural Styles for Multi-Carrier Integration}

Based on the literature and the guiding principles discussed above, several architectural styles can be considered for implementing a multi-carrier integration layer.
The following list of candidates is neither exhaustive of all possible options, nor are some of the choices seriously considered for implementation, but they still serve as useful points of comparison to understand trade-offs.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., left=0pt, itemsep=3pt]
  \item Point-to-point integration
  \item Hub-and-spoke broker
  \item Message-oriented service bus
  \item API gateway-centric architecture
  \item Event-driven microservices
\end{enumerate}

The following subsections describe each of these in turn, discuss their advantages and disadvantages, and relate them to the goals of this thesis.

\subsection{Point-to-Point Integration}
In a point-to-point architecture, the application establishes direct connections to each external system. For the thesis project, this would mean that the backend directly invokes the shipping provider APIs wherever the functionality is needed, often with branching logic such as:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,caption={Point-to-point integration example pseudocode},label=lst:carrier-dispatch-example]
if carrier == "DHL": call_dhl_api(...)
elif carrier == "GLS": call_gls_api(...)
...
\end{lstlisting}

This approach is simple and often used in early-stage prototypes because it has a low upfront cost and development time, by saving time on additional infrastructure or architectural components.
Actually, this approach was once utilized at the start of the project, up until two carriers were integrated, to quickly validate and develop other core functionalities, which I will discuss in future chapters.
Clearly, this is not very scalable, nor does it promote any criteria of extensibility discussed prior.
Literature also consistently identifies point-to-point integration as problematic at scale, even describing it as an expensive approach and bad extreme on the scale of integration \cite{gulledgeWhatIntegration2006}. 
Every new integration increases the number of dependencies and potential failure points, and changes in one system can ripple unpredictably through others.
In the context of \textit{Shopickup}, the main disadvantages of point-to-point integration, as experienced firsthand and reported in literature, include:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{High coupling}: The core application must “know” about the details of every carrier API, violating SOA principles and making change localized and difficult.
  \item \textbf{Poor extensibility}: Adding a new carrier involves editing central business logic and testing the entire application, not just an isolated module.
  \item \textbf{Limited reuse}: Any normalization or error-handling logic gets duplicated across multiple code paths.
\end{itemize}

For these reasons, point-to-point integration can serve as a baseline or initial stage but is not a suitable architecture for the extensible multi-carrier platform targeted in this thesis.

\subsection{Hub-and-Spoke (Central Broker)}

A hub-and-spoke architecture introduces a dedicated broker component that sits between the core application and the external systems.
The core application communicates only with the broker using a canonical data format. The broker then routes and transforms messages to and from individual spokes, which are the carrier adapters.
In the projects context, the broker would:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., left=0pt, itemsep=3pt]
  \item Accept generic shipping requests such as \verb|CreateShipment(order, carrier)| in a normalized format.
  \item Select the appropriate carrier adapter based on the requested carrier or routing logic.
  \item Transform the canonical request into the carrier's specific format and invoke the carrier API.
  \item Transform the carrier's response back into the canonical format before returning it to the core application.
\end{enumerate}

This architecture is closer to SOA principles and facilitates the use of Adapter and normalization patterns.
It brings several benefits:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item The core application is decoupled from carrier details and depends only on the broker's canonical interface.
  \item All data transformation is centralized in the broker, improving consistency.
  \item It becomes easier to add logging, basic rate limiting, or validation in one place.
\end{itemize}

However, this approach also has drawbacks.
The broker can become a single point of failure and a performance bottleneck, especially if all traffic is routed synchronously through it, scaling is complex.
In practice, hub-and-spoke architectures are often a stepping stone towards more distributed patterns such as service buses or microservices with API gateways.
When contemplating the architecture for the system, after seeing the limitations of point-to-point integration, this pattern felt like a natural next step.

\subsection{Message-Oriented Service Bus}

A more decoupled architecture uses a message-oriented middleware or service bus, where components communicate via asynchronous messages, often in a publish-subscribe model via message queues or topics.
This approach is widely applied in enterprise integration and IoT middleware, where the asynchronous nature of communication is not a limitation.
In our practical example, a service bus architecture might work as follows:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item The core application publishes a \verb|CreateShipment| message to a queue or topic, containing the order data and the selected carrier.
  \item One or more carrier adapter services subscribe to these messages. Each adapter handles messages for its specific carrier, performing the necessary API calls and transformations.
  \item Once a label is created, the adapter publishes a \verb|ShipmentCreated| event, which the core application or other services consume to update their state or trigger notifications.
\end{itemize}

This approach has apparent advantages in terms of loose coupling, scalability, resilience and throughput.
The disadvantages are mostly related to complexity:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item System behavior becomes asynchronous and eventually consistent; labels may not be available immediately after the order is placed.
  \item Debugging and tracing flows across multiple queues and services is more complex than direct synchronous invocation.
  \item Operating and securing a message bus comes with its own operational challenges and complexities.
\end{itemize}

Given the scope of a bachelor thesis and the requirement for near-real-time feedback in this e-commerce workflow (e.g., changing order states instantly), a fully asynchronous service bus architecture is likely too heavy and unnecessarily complex for our purposes and is more suitable on the higher level on a general e-commerce platform architecture, such as in Baraka and Al-Ashqar's paper \cite{barakaBuildingSOABasedModel2013}. 
However, other specific parts of the system, such as background tracking updates, scheduled tasks such as pickup location synchronization, or analytics data processing, could benefit from message-oriented designs in future iterations.

\subsection{API Gateway-Centric Multi-Carrier Architecture}

The API Gateway pattern offers a pragmatic compromise between the simplicity of direct integration and the flexibility and extensibility of more advanced service-oriented approaches.
It is particularly well-suited for systems that expose a unified API to clients while internally delegating work to multiple backend services.
It would work as follows in our context:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item The gateway exposes a stable REST API to the frontend and other consumers.
  \item Each carrier integration is implemented as a separate service (Adapter) that adheres to a shared internal interface.
  \item The gateway routes requests to the appropriate adapter based on routing rules (e.g., selected carrier, destination).
  \item The gateway can also perform cross-cutting tasks such as authentication, rate limiting, logging, and caching.
\end{itemize}

This architecture combines the benefits of the Adapter pattern with the centralized control of an API Gateway.
Compared to hub-and-spoke, the gateway approach more naturally aligns with microservices and modern DevOps tooling, and commercial and open-source gateway solutions (e.g., Kong, NGINX) are mature and well-supported.
At the same time, it avoids some of the operational complexity of full event-driven microservice architectures.

\subsection{Event-Driven Microservices}

The most advanced architecture considered in the literature combines an API Gateway with a fully event-driven microservice back end. In such systems, individual business capabilities—Orders, Shipping, Tracking, Notifications—are each implemented as independent services that communicate via events on a message bus (e.g., Kafka).
The advantages of this style include:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item High scalability and fault tolerance: each service can be scaled independently, and failures in one area (e.g., tracking updates) do not necessarily affect others.
  \item Fine-grained deployment and evolution: services can be updated and re-deployed independently.
  \item Fault tolerance and resilience: services can retry operations, handle backpressure, and recover from transient failures more gracefully.
\end{itemize}

However, systematic reviews of microservice systems also highlight the increased architectural complexity, the need for sophisticated monitoring and testing, and the challenge of managing eventual consistency across many services \cite{benaventeComparativeAnalysisMicroservices2022}. 
These factors make full event-driven microservice architectures more appropriate for large organizations with substantial engineering resources (e.g., Amazon, Netflix, Uber) than for SME-oriented projects or bachelor thesis prototypes.
For this thesis, event-driven microservices are therefore considered primarily as a reference point for future evolution rather than as the immediate target architecture.

\section{Preexisting Solutions and Papers in this Domain}
As previously mentioned, there is a lack of academic literature directly addressing multi-carrier shipping integration at the API level.
Most relevant conference paper found is by Lin et al. in 2010, in which they similarly first emphasize the gap in this research topic, then follow up by proposing a SOA guided, J2EE based framework for integrating shipping providers \cite{linResearchShippingECommerce2010}.
While their work is conceptually aligned with the principles discussed earlier, especially regarding coupling and extensibility, as also mentioned by the authors themselves, their implementation is now somewhat dated.
The industry has since largely moved towards lightweight RESTful APIs and microservice architectures, not to mention the replacement of J2EE and XML-based web services with more modern frameworks.
Therefore, while Lin et al.'s work provides a useful historical perspective and validation of SOA principles and wrapper patterns, even including message brokers, it does not directly influence the design of a contemporary system like \textit{Shopickup}.

Most other existing literature in the domain are more general and aim to theorize a broader e-commerce architecture.
These can still be relevant to some extent, as they often highlight the same challenges and propose similar SOA-based solutions, or may even address logistics and shipping as part of a larger system.
Good examples include the following works:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item The paper of Bhakar and Al-Ashqar, in which they propose an enterprise service bus as the central integration layer for their purchase order management system, with a separate shipping service module \cite{barakaBuildingSOABasedModel2013}. 
  \item Xiong-yi similarly proposes SOA as the better alternative for e-commerce systems and highlights the heterogeneity of external systems, as we have also seen before in a more general context with APIs \cite{xiong-yiResearchApplicationSOA2009}. 
  \item Li's paper confirms that the adapter method is now a standard approach for encapsulating legacy service interfaces in modern SOA e-commerce platforms \cite{liDesignB2BEcommerce2019}. 
\end{itemize}

It is also worth mentioning that some similar commercial and even open-source solutions do exist to what we are trying to achieve, however they are often fully fledged applications with limited extensibility, such as \textit{karrio} \cite{KarrioapiKarrio2026}, or are indeed lightweight, but limited to specific vendors or use-cases and follow more of a point-to-point approach, such as \textit{ShippingProAPICollection} \cite{kevinvenclovasKevinvenclovasShippingProAPICollection2025}. 

\section{Design, Evaluation and Analytics of Shipping Monitoring Systems}
The second half of my literature review focuses on the more usability focused aspect of the application with the following two research questions in mind:

\begin{enumerate}[resume, label=\textbf{MRQ2}, left=0pt, itemsep=3pt]
  \item How can a data analytics dashboard be designed such that merchants receive actionable insights into shipping performance and customer preferences while balancing usability, scalability, and real-time needs?
        \begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{SRQ2.1}, left=1.8em, itemsep=3pt]
          \item How can the effectiveness of such a system in shipping operations be evaluated, which KPIs are most relevant to merchants?
        \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

Analytical dashboards and monitoring systems are widely used in various domains, ranging from IoT to e-commerce platforms.
Their purpose is to provide users with a clear overview of key metrics, trends and anomalies, enabling informed decision-making.
However, it is not immediately clear how to best design and evaluate such a system, that is both efficient and provides real value to merchants.
We can not just simply measure system performance in just technical terms, such as the Google SRE team's Four Golden Signals \cite{GoogleSREMonitoring} (latency, traffic, errors, saturation), but also need to consider user-centric and domain specific metrics as well.
The following subsections explore relevant literature on dashboard design architectures, KPIs and evaluation methods in the context of shipping monitoring systems.

\subsection{On the Importance of Technical Metrics}
Technical infrastructure metrics such as uptime percentages, query response latencies, and system throughput represent necessary conditions for effective monitoring systems but are not sufficient indicators of value.
A shipping monitoring dashboard with near perfect uptime and zero latency means little if the presented information is not understandable or actionable.
Thus, evaluation of monitoring systems must be addressed on both technical and user-centric dimensions.

Infrastructure metrics often simply serve as enablers of business value rather than measures of value themselves.
Google's Four Golden Signals framework \cite{GoogleSREMonitoring} provides useful infrastructure monitoring patterns, but requires contextualization for shipping domains:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Latency} for example in case of our application is not as crucial as in some other real-time systems, such as financial trading platforms. The main importance of latency in our case is more about user experience when merchants interact with the dashboard, rather than strict real-time constraints.
  \item \textbf{Traffic and throughput} indicate system load but reveal nothing about whether the dashboard drives better merchant decisions. It is however, an important factor when considering scalability, as in this case we must respect external API rate limits, such as carrier APIs or Shopify's Admin API.
  \item \textbf{Error rates} measure system reliability, and are critical for both trustworthiness and usability. It must be monitored closely with each added integration and feature.
  \item \textbf{Saturation} indicates when scaling is needed but not whether the system generates merchant value. It is also not much of a concern in our case, as there are not many computationally intensive operations, and due to the web technologies used, scaling is often handled automatically by cloud providers.
\end{itemize}

Evidently, in our case, while technical metrics are important for ensuring reliable operation, what we are really looking for are user-centric KPIs that reflect the dashboard's effectiveness in driving merchant decisions.

\subsection{Real-Time vs. Batch Analytics}
\label{sec:real-time-vs-batch-analytics}
Now let us address the main topic of \textbf{MRQ2}, which is the architectural and design choice of our proposed dashboard system.
A fundamental architectural decision in shipping monitoring systems is the choice between real-time and batch analytics approaches.
This decision is not primarily technical but rather reflects what decision-making problems, time horizons and type of data analysis the system aims to support.

\subsubsection{Operational Intelligence vs. Business Intelligence and Their Different Needs}
To understand the distinction, we first need to clarify the difference between operational intelligence and business intelligence (BI).
For this, I turn to Sandu's definitions \cite{sanduOperationalRealtimeBusiness2008}:

\begin{displayquote}
  "BI enables business users to report on, analyze and optimize business operations to reduce costs and increase revenues. Organizations use BI for strategic and tactical decision making where the decision-making cycle may span a time period of several weeks or months.
  ... there is now a need to use BI to help drive and optimize business operations on a daily basis ...  usually called operational business intelligence and real-time business intelligence." \parencite{sanduOperationalRealtimeBusiness2008}
\end{displayquote}

Batch analytics usually processes accumulated data in scheduled bulk operations, enabling comprehensive analysis of patterns over longer time horizons and greater amounts of data.
A batch job that runs weekly might analyze all shipments of the given week to give greater insight on carrier performance, cost trends, or customer preferences.

Operational intelligence focuses on current situations, and thus they require low-latency, real-time data processing.
Real-time analytics processes data continuously as events occur, enabling decisions in motion while situations are still unfolding.
This rapid response window—often measured in minutes rather than hours—enables operational intelligence, acting on immediate situations, such as shipment delays or inventory shortages.

These two intelligence types serve different decision problems and cannot easily substitute for each other.
The architectural implications are profound as well.
On one hand, real-time systems prioritize latency by accepting operational complexity. When discussing big data scale, real-time analytics are especially hard, as evidenced by the research of Zheng et al. \cite{zhengRealtimeBigData2025} in which they find that real-time big data systems must simultaneously satisfy strict timeliness, high stability and availability on different levels of scale which leads to substantially higher system complexity compared to traditional batch-oriented data warehouses.
On the other hand, batch systems prioritize analytical comprehensiveness by accepting latency. Data is extracted from operational systems, transformed through staged pipelines, and in case of great amounts of data loaded into dimensional data warehouses where complex SQL queries execute efficiently.
Historical data is preserved for deep analysis. However, data arrives with significant latency and usually based on scheduled intervals, limiting responsiveness to immediate disruptions.
A batch system discovering that a carrier had a 20\% delay rate last week due to issues specific to it is useful for strategic planning, but too late for immediate action on current shipments such as turning off the carrier or switching to an alternative.

It is apparent, that most business use cases require both types of intelligence and thus the corresponding analytical methods and architectures.
A good way to decide between real-time and batch analytics for given problems is by considering the decision-window requirements for them.

Here are two illustrative use cases for shipping monitoring systems:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item\textbf{Real-time use case (Operational Intelligence)}: Shipment Service Level Agreement (SLA) breach alerts. Shipping carriers often offer promised delivery windows, such as "delivery within 2 business days", and merchants need to be able to monitor these SLAs, both to proactively manage customer expectations and to take corrective actions against carriers when breaches occur.
  \item\textbf{Batch use case (Business Intelligence)}: Weekly carrier performance reports. Merchants need to analyze carrier performance over longer periods to inform strategic decisions about carrier selection and customer preferences, contract negotiations, and logistics planning.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Lambda Architecture as a Hybrid Reference Model}
To formalize the hybrid approach described above, we turn to the Lambda Architecture, a data-processing design pattern introduced by Nathan Marz \cite{marzHowBeatCAP2011} to handle massive quantities of data by taking advantage of both batch and stream-processing methods.
While originally conceived to address the complexities of “Big Data” and the CAP theorem (Consistency, Availability, and Partition Tolerance) in distributed systems, the core principles of the Lambda Architecture provide a robust reference model for this project's monitoring system, even at a smaller scale.
Marz argues that traditional databases conflate two distinct problems: storage of immutable facts and the computation of views (queries) on those facts \cite{marzHowBeatCAP2011}. 
In purely real-time or mutable systems, ensuring consistency while maintaining availability (as per the CAP theorem) introduces significant complexity, such as read-repair logic, vector clocks, and the risk of permanent data corruption from buggy writes.
To “beat” this complexity, the Lambda Architecture proposes separating the system into three distinct layers, a structure that aligns directly with the hybrid requirements identified in the previous section.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., left=0pt, itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Batch Layer (Master Dataset)}: Marz defines the “master dataset” as an immutable, append-only set of raw data \cite{marzHowBeatCAP2011}. Because data is never overwritten, human errors or buggy code cannot permanently corrupt the system, views can simply be recomputed. In the context of shipping monitoring, this corresponds to the nightly storage of all raw shipment events and carrier updates. This layer precomputes complex, heavy views (e.g., weekly carrier performance reports) that don't need to be up-to-the-second but must be accurate and comprehensive.
  \item \textbf{Speed Layer (Real-Time)}: Since batch views are inherently out of date by the duration of the batch interval, a separate "Speed Layer" compensates for this "data lag" \cite{marzHowBeatCAP2011}. This layer processes only the most recent data (the delta) to provide low-latency views. Marz notes that while this layer requires complex incremental algorithms and eventual consistency, any errors here are transient—they are eventually overwritten by the correct Batch Layer views.
  \item \textbf{Serving Layer}: "The serving layer pulls together the results from both the speed and batch layers, which makes an easy single view for the applications and users, which consume the data." as defined by Lekkala \cite{lekkalaLeveragingLambdaArchitecture2020}.
\end{enumerate}

While Marz's original proposal suggests heavy industrial technologies like Apache Hadoop for the speed layer, the pattern is technology-agnostic, which is made apparent by how different papers implement it or suggest implementing it via different technologies \cite{lekkalaLeveragingLambdaArchitecture2020, hasaniLambdaArchitectureReal2014}. 
For a small-sized, focused application like ours, strictly adhering to these “Big Data” technologies would introduce unnecessary operational overhead, which is a common issue noted by critics, such as Krep who points out that maintaining two parallel codebases (one for batch, one for streaming) for the same purposes violates the “Don't Repeat Yourself” (DRY) principle and increases maintenance costs \cite{krepsQuestioningLambdaArchitecture2014}. 
However, the functional separation remains valid and valuable. Instead of Hadoop and Storm, such applications could implement a lightweight approach that takes inspiration from the Lambda architecture.

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Batch Layer (Master Dataset)}: can be implemented effectively using standard relational database aggregations or scheduled cron jobs that run during off-peak hours to generate static reporting tables, which later could be replaced by a more robust ETL pipeline if needed.
  \item \textbf{Speed Layer (Real-Time)}: does not require a complex event-streaming engine (like Kafka) if the data volume does not demand it. Instead, frequent polling of carrier APIs (e.g., every 15 minutes, depending on the provider's limits) or lightweight webhook handlers if available can serve the same functional purpose: capturing the latest state to alert merchants of immediate issues.
  \item \textbf{Serving Layer}: can be a simple API that merges results from both layers, serving real-time alerts from the Speed Layer and historical reports from the Batch Layer to the dashboard.
\end{itemize}

Based on the literature research this hybrid approach seems to be a suitable candidate for our project, is further explored in the corresponding subsection \ref{sec:dashboard-design} of the methodology.
Were the scope of the project larger, Lambda's alternative, the Kappa architecture proposed by Kreps \cite{krepsQuestioningLambdaArchitecture2014} or even a CQRS + Event Sourcing approach \cite{pachecoMicroservicePatternsBest2018} might be considered as better alternatives.

\subsection{Domain-Specific KPIs for Shipping Operations}
With understanding of the real-time/batch distinction established, we can now continue with the \textbf{SRQ2.1} and define domain-specific KPIs appropriate for merchant evaluation and decision-making.
For this, we primarily rely on the standard of \textbf{SCOR DS} (Supply Chain Operations Reference Digital Standard) set forth by the \textit{Association for Supply Chain Management} (ASCM), that describes and categorizes a framework of over \texttt{250} KPIs relevant to supply chain management \cite{ascm2022scor}. 
While not all of these KPIs are relevant to our specific domain, it still provides a comprehensive reference for core metrics and serves as the focal point of many related papers, such as the paper of Kusrini \& Miranda that addresses make-to-order scenarios, which is especially common for \textit{Shopify} SMEs \cite{kusriniDeterminingPerformanceMetrics2021}.

There are many ways to categorize KPIs, and the literature does not provide a single standard.
For instance, \textbf{SCOR DS} categorizes them into distinct hierarchies of process categories (Plan, Order, Source, Transform, Fulfill, and Return), while Piela organized by factors most relevant to the company, such as whether they are financial or non-financial, primary or secondary metrics and so on \cite{ascm2022scor, pielaKeyPerformanceIndicator2017}. 
We however, categorize them into tiers reflecting the different decision horizons they support, as described earlier.
This serves both to clarify their purpose and to guide the underlying data architecture needed to support them in practice.

While SCOR DS provides an exhaustive dictionary of metrics, recent literature emphasizes that SMEs require a simplified, capability-linked subset of these KPIs that focus heavily on customer value outcomes and unit economics rather than complex network utilization \cite{kusriniDeterminingPerformanceMetrics2021, llivisaca2020key}. 
For an SME merchant fulfilling orders, the focal point shifts toward end-to-end metrics like Total Order Cycle Time (TOCT) and Cost Per Order (CPO).
Implementation of some of these KPIs is attempted in section \ref{sec:dashboard-design}, followed by an evaluation of their effectiveness based on merchant feedback in the form of two in-depth interviews.

Operation and strategic KPIs are not mutually exclusive, but rather complementary representations of the same underlying data.
Operational KPIs can usually be turned into strategic ones by simply aggregating them over time.
Similarly, we can make multiple versions of our strategic KPIs by aggregating them over different time horizons, such as weekly, monthly, quarterly or yearly and by grouping them by different shipment providers.
For the practical implementation, differentiating between carriers is especially important, as merchants often have multiple carriers and need to compare their performance, reliability and costs.

\subsection{Core Issue: Specificity vs. Generality}
One of the main challenges in designing the core contract was finding the right balance of what should be included in the core and what should be left out.
On one hand, the core should be general enough to be applicable to a wide range of carriers, but as we have seen throughout the literature and in practice, these APIs are not uniform at all, with each carrier having their own unique requirements and quirks.
On the other hand, the core should be specific enough to provide real value and guidance to adapter developers to avoid regressing to the one-to-one mapping antipattern.

The compromise for this issue was to define the contract in a way where the types provide a base structure with predefined points for extension on the input and raw response field for the result.
Perhaps, the best way to understand this is through an example, such as the \texttt{createParcel} flow mentioned before.
On the input side, the core defines a \texttt{CreateParcelRequest} type with the usual expected fields for the incoming data, such as \texttt{Parcel}.
It is apparent however, that different carriers offer unique features and services that require additional fields, such as express delivery flags, insurance options and so on.
To accommodate this, the core defines a custom field in the type of \texttt{RequestOptions}, which is deliberately left up to the adapter to define and use as they see fit.

\begin{listing}[htbp]
  \caption{Core request options extensibility in \texttt{src/core/request-options.ts}}
  \label{lst:request-options}
  \begin{minted}[fontsize=\small, linenos, breaklines, frame=single]{typescript}
/**
 * Request options
 * Per-call options that adapters can use to modify behavior
 */
export interface RequestOptions {
  /**
   * Use test/sandbox API endpoint instead of production
   * Some carriers have separate test APIs (e.g., Foxpost: webapi-test.foxpost.hu)
   * Default: false
   */
  useTestApi?: boolean;

  /**
   * Custom options for future extensibility
   */
  [key: string]: unknown;
}
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

This lenient typing allows adapters to first extend the core types, then specify them to their needs, such as the following example for Foxpost's parcel creation, which extends the (aliased) core type we have seen before and specifies the \texttt{options} field with custom flags, such as \texttt{isWeb}, that indicates to the carrier whether the parcel shall be hidden in their own interface.

\begin{listing}[htbp]
  \caption{Adapter, Foxpost-specific request options in \texttt{src/adapters/foxpost/types.ts}}
  \label{lst:foxpost-request-options}
  \begin{minted}[fontsize=\small, linenos, breaklines, frame=single]{typescript}
/**
 * Foxpost-specific CreateParcelsRequest (narrowed credentials)
 */
export interface CreateParcelsRequestFoxpost extends CoreCreateParcelsRequest {
  credentials: FoxpostCredentials;
  options?: FoxpostParcelOptions;
}

/**
 * Foxpost-specific parcel options.
 *
 * `useTestApi` controls the base URL selection.
 * `foxpost.isWeb` and `foxpost.isRedirect` map to Foxpost parcel query flags.
 */
export interface FoxpostParcelOptions extends RequestOptions {
  foxpost?: {
    isWeb?: boolean;
    isRedirect?: boolean;
  };
}
\end{minted}
\end{listing}

Similarly, with the responses, the core defines a base structure for the output that includes all sensible and common fields, such as \texttt{trackingNumber} for instance in case of parcel creation, but integrators might need more details returned by the carriers.
For this reason, the core defines \texttt{rawCarrierResponse} as a field in the output, which is simply a free-form serialized version of the carrier's original response body, allowing integrators access to all details they might need without locking in to the core's defined structure.

\subsection{Test-driven Approach}
Right from the start of development, a test-driven approach was favored for the carrier adapters especially, as it has multiple benefits for both the long term reliability of the adapter and the development process itself.
First, it ensures that the implementation is correct, and in case of truly end-to-end testing, the tests can at any time be repeated to see whether the implementation is still valid and working as expected.
Second, it serves as a form of documentation for the adapter, as the tests describe the expected behavior and edge cases of the implementation.

As can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:integration-layer-carrier-adapters-file-structure}, the tests are organized in a separate directory with another level of separation by the depth of concern.

\section{Dashboard Design Framework}
\label{sec:dashboard-design}
This section describes the specifics of how the dashboard was implemented, what technologies were used, and the challenges faced during development.
Development of the dashboard was done in parallel with the rest of the application, and it was designed to be a central hub for merchants to monitor their shipping operations and gain insights into their performance.

First concern was to define the order of components and the layout while keeping in mind the need for flexibility and customization.
The final design was a 3 tiered layout:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Top bar}: serves as a constant point of reference, always showing data related to the merchant's account, their subscription plan and reassurance that the system is working as expected. It also holds some application-wide settings and navigation options, such as a language selector and link to the latest orders.

  \item \textbf{Main section}: The main section of the dashboard is a grid of tiles, each tile representing a KPI or metric that is relevant to the merchant's shipping operations. The tiles can be rearranged according to the merchant's preferences, and they can choose from a variety of visualizations, such as line charts, bar charts, pie charts, and more through a modal. This uses a three columns wide base layout and the tiles range from one to two columns wide by one row. The design is also responsive, on vertical mobile views it breaks down to a two-column layout and tiles are reorganized accordingly.

\subsection{Batch Layer}

The Batch Layer follows the Lambda Architecture pattern described in Section \ref{sec:real-time-vs-batch-analytics} and implements the master-dataset concept via a nightly cron job that populates a dedicated aggregate table, \texttt{ShipmentDailyAggregate}.
For every shop, the cron queries all shipments that had activity on the previous calendar day and groups them by shipping provider and shipping type.
For each group a numerous set of aggregate metrics is computed, including counts, sums, and averages of the KPIs or data utilized for their computation.

The aggregate rows are upserted so the cron is essentially idempotent. Re-running it for a given date and carrier overwrites the previous values rather than duplicating them, consistent with Marz's principle that batch views can always be recomputed from the immutable source data \cite{marzHowBeatCAP2011}. 
This design eliminates expensive N+1 queries on the raw shipment table at dashboard render time.
Instead, the Batch Layer serves all historical KPIs by reading directly from the precomputed aggregate table via simple \texttt{GROUP BY} and \texttt{SUM} queries.
Even cross-carrier comparisons and multi-period views (e.g., 90-day daily orders, 12-month return rate trend) require only a handful of indexed reads rather than scanning thousands of shipment rows.

\subsection{Speed Layer}

The Speed Layer compensates for the inherent latency of the nightly batch interval by querying live database tables on every dashboard load.
It is responsible for metrics where freshness matters more than long-term comprehensiveness, similar to how the warning and message system prioritizes timeliness over historical completeness.

This layer deliberately trades some query complexity for timeliness: the computations run synchronously in the dashboard metrics loader and reflect the state of the database at request time.
Because the raw shipment table is queried for SLA and OTDR metrics, the Speed Layer guarantees that merchants see breaches and compliance figures almost as soon as they are apparent in our systems, supporting the operational intelligence use cases identified in Section. \ref{sec:real-time-vs-batch-analytics}
The design of components served through the speed layer needs to be carefully considered, as to not overload the database with expensive queries even if merchants decide to use all available tiles on the dashboard.

\subsection{Serving Layer}

The Serving Layer is a single API endpoint, \texttt{/api/dashboard-metrics}, that merges the results from both layers into one \textit{JSON} response structured as three blocks.

\begin{enumerate}[left=0pt, itemsep=3pt]
  \item \texttt{speed}: all real-time KPIs, similarly surfaced to warnings and messages
  \item \texttt{batch}: all historically aggregated KPIs from the nightly cron job that are directly utilized
  \item \texttt{computed}: KPIs primarily derived from batch aggregates but presented separately for clarity (e.g., perfect order rate, carrier cost statistics and per-carrier OTDR).
\end{enumerate}

Each response also carries a \texttt{\_meta} object with two timestamps: \texttt{speedFreshAt} (the time the live queries ran) and \texttt{batchLastDate} (the most recent date present in the aggregate table, or \texttt{null} if the cron has never run).
On the dashboard, every tile displays a freshness note in its tooltip - \textit{“Live data”} for Speed Layer tiles and \textit{“Batch data: [date]”} for Batch Layer tiles - giving the merchant immediate awareness of data recency.
Data is lazily loaded via the previously mentioned endpoint, utilizing a separate fetcher as to not block the main application render and allow for a more responsive user experience.

This design mirrors the Lambda Architecture's separation of concerns as described by Lekkala: the batch layer is authoritative and eventually consistent, the speed layer fills the freshness gap, and the serving layer presents a unified view that transparently informs the user about which data source powers each metric \cite{lekkalaLeveragingLambdaArchitecture2020}.

\section{Integration Layer Implementation}
\subsubsection{Integration Layer - Core}
As mentioned before, the core contract is the most abstract element of the system. It serves the following purposes:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Abstract capability and flow definitions}: it defines the capabilities carrier adapters can implement, such as label creation, parcel creation, tracking, etc. Adapters can then choose which of these capabilities they implement based on the carrier's available API endpoints and clearly advertise them as available. Taking this idea one step further, the core can also define flows that combine multiple capabilities into higher-level sequences, such as "create label" that would first create a parcel, then print its label in a singular step.
  \item \textbf{Canonical models and type safety}: the whole project was built with \textit{TypeScript} and is strongly typed, the core accordingly defines the canonical domain models via base types for the adapters. The adapters import these types, extend them with carrier-specific fields where required and return the output in a similar predefined format. This enforces a certain level of consistency across adapters and promotes predictability. In the following figure, we can see for example through the example of a parcel creation flow how the core defines strict types such as \texttt{Parcel}, \texttt{CreateParcelsRequest} and \texttt{CreateParcelResponse}, defining the core types end to end.
  \item \textbf{Pluggable central utilities and context}: the core also provides crucial utilities used by adapters, such as HTTP clients, logging, telemetry or store helpers. As of the time of writing, the http client and logging utilities are completely implemented and used. The core discipline is to keep these utilities generic and pluggable, the package does provide for example a simply initializable \textit{Axios} / \textit{Fetch} based HTTP client, but developers also have the choice to use their own custom client as long as it adheres to or is mappable to the core's defined standard HTTP interface type. Similarly, the logging utility provides a simple \textit{pino} logger setup, but implementers can bring their own logger instances if they can map to the standard info/debug/error level logs. The adapters then use these utilities for their internal operations.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Integration Layer - Carrier Adapters}
Carrier adapters are the concrete implementations of the core contract for each carrier.
They all have a rather predictable structure, which is recommended to be followed by guidelines for new implementations.
Here is an example structure for a carrier adapter in Figure \ref{fig:integration-layer-carrier-adapters-file-structure}, excluding miscellaneous files, helpers and directories.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.20\textwidth]{src/diagrams/integration-layer-carrier-adapters-file-structure.png}
  \caption{File structure of the integration layer - carrier adapters}
  \label{fig:integration-layer-carrier-adapters-file-structure}
\end{figure}

The main functionalities that define and differentiate the adapters are the following:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Capabilities}: One file recommended per each group of capability exposed by the carrier. Defines the actual functions that utilize the pluggable context, such as the HTTP client and logger to then make requests to the carrier's API and return the normalized output. Orchestrates from the mapping of the core's canonical domain model to the carrier's expected request, handles the API call according to the carrier's specification and expected format (e.g., JSON or XML), response, potential errors, and finally maps the carrier's response back to the core's canonical output format.
  \item \textbf{Mappers}: Are imported as functions by the capability functions and are responsible for the mapping to and from the core's canonical domain model. It would be easy to just think of them as simple field mappers, but they do actually hold functionality, which should as per the documentation guidelines set forth be described in detail. One of the best examples for parcel creation is package sizes, most carriers have predefined package size categories, such as small, medium and large, defined by the dimensions and weight of the parcel. The mappers in this case can implement logic from provided documentations and based on the incoming core \texttt{Parcel} data, automatically determine the correct size category (unless deliberately overridden via custom options).
  \item \textbf{Types}: A central types file imports the core types, extends them with carrier-specific fields where needed and then these are used by both the mappers and validators to ensure type safety and the core to adapter contract changeover.
  \item \textbf{Validation}: This is another crucial part of the adapters, here we define \textbf{Zod} schemas to be used for validation throughout the capability functions. These are used to safely validate input variables beyond just the type (e.g., for strings length or format such as email), throughout the process to validate that the carrier's response is as expected as per the documentation, and also to validate the final output before returning results. Essentially, while the type safe approach throughout the project ensure developers have good and consistent experience when building or utilizing the packages, the validation ensures actual runtime safety and predictability of the system.
  \item \textbf{Tests}: Each adapter comes with its own set of tests, which are crucial for ensuring the reliability and correctness of implementation. The tests cover various scenarios across different levels of concern, but since this is a big part of the adapter development's design philosophy, we will go over it in more detail in the next subsection.
\end{itemize}

Main design choice with the adapters was to keep them simple and focused on implementing the core contract in the provider's context.
This way, the contracts and already built implementations serve as a clear guide for new implementations, significantly reducing effort and time needed to add a new carrier.

\section{End Application - API Gateway and Tracking Service}
As mentioned before, the API gateway and tracking service are the most concrete and application-specific components of the system.
These are completely dependent on the end application and its requirements, and as such, they are not part of the integration layer itself.

For the use case of the \textit{Shopickup} application the API gateways are rather simple, mostly acting as routing layers, since we do not have to create sophisticated backoff mechanisms against rate limiting, as for each call we are using the merchant's own credentials.
There are two main capabilities that are implemented through this architecture: parcel creation and tracking.

Parcel creation is a straightforward flow, where the API gateway receives a request from the frontend for one or multiple parcels, validates it, and then routes it to the appropriate carrier adapters based on what carrier shipping methods were selected for the orders.
It calls the adapters' parcel and label creation functions, then aggregates the results and returns them to the frontend in a unified format.
This aggregation is made easy by the core's canonical domain model, which ensures that the output from each adapter adheres to a consistent structure.

Tracking service runs through cron job that is periodically invoked to first fetch which shipments are due for tracking, then calls the adapters' tracking functions for each shipment and updates the database with the latest tracking information.
Here the main cross-cutting concern is to manage for each carrier and order the different tracking intervals allowed by the carriers.
The shipping carrier MPL for example recommends only tracking and order once every 6 hours, while other carriers such as Foxpost allow for more frequent tracking.

\section{Developer Tooling}
The developer tooling provided for the integration layer is designed to facilitate the development and testing of carrier adapters, as well as to provide a reference implementation for integrators and developers.
It is a command-line interface (CLI) tool that allows developers to quickly define locally callable scripts of their newly created adapter capabilities.
These functions are really helpful for testing and debugging, as they allow developers to quickly call the adapter's functions with different inputs and see the results without having to implement them in a full application context.

The CLI tool does not simply route and call static predefined scripts, but instead defines a smart context for the functions to be called in.
For example, developers have the choice to directly call it via CLI input, or make predefined \texttt{JSON} fixture files that define the input for repeatable testing and documentation purposes.
It offers optional convenient helpers, such as the one for authorization, reading in credentials from environment variables and injecting them into specific keys of the request object to avoid hardcoding them in the fixture files.
There are further utilities that help with validating the output, such as the built-in configurable \textit{pino} logger with which developers can see the output in a structured and readable format, or the option to write the output to a file for further inspection.
Writing the output to a file is especially useful in case of label generation, where the tool has flag for writing the received shipping label to PDF.

Furthermore, a lightweight \textit{Fastify} server is also provided in the repository, through which developers may document and expose their adapter's capabilities in a visual format, with the ability to call them directly from the browser.
This is done through \textit{OpenAPI} documentation and a \textit{Swagger UI} interface, which is automatically generated from the OpenAPI specifications.
At the moment of writing, this is simply a proof of concept and has not been fully implemented or kept up to date for each carrier, as development is more convenient via the CLI tool, but it is a promising direction for future development showcasing capabilities and serving as central documentation hub for all carrier adapters.

\section{Shopickup Application Overview}
\textit{Shopickup} is a multi-carrier shipping integration and monitoring platform designed for e-commerce merchants, that implements the integration layer as packages and builds an application around it that follows the principles set forth in the literature review.
It allows merchants to extend their online stores with a unified interface for managing shipments across multiple carriers, print labels, track packages, and gain insights into shipping performance through a dedicated dashboard.
Customers of these stores also have the benefit of utilizing the platform's pickup point picker widget, which provides them with a seamless experience for selecting their preferred delivery options during checkout.

Since this is a commercial application with proprietary license, unlike the integration layer itself, the source code will not be shared. Instead, a high level overview of it is provided, only going into greater detail for the parts relevant to the thesis's main research questions.
While the app itself does not directly answer any of our research questions, it serves as context on how the proposed carrier integration system can be applied in the real world, and also as a vector for me to explore the design and evaluation of the monitoring dashboard.
First an overview of the common user flows is provided as context, followed by a description of the architectural choices and technologies used, paying special attention to the parts relevant to the thesis.

\subsection{Architectural Overview and Technology Stack}
\label{sec:shopickup-architecture-overview}
Now that we have seen the user flows of the application, we can go into the architectural details of where the carrier integration layer plugs into the application, and also to give an overview of the technologies used and effort required to build such a system.
Most of the technology stack consists of modern standard tools and frameworks that are commonly used in the industry, however in some specific regards the choices have been limited or influenced by Shopify's platform requirements for embedded apps.

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Backend + Frontend}: The backend + frontend is specified by Shopify to be built using \textbf{React Router v7} in the server-side rendering framework mode, which means that for small applications such as this one, both the backend and frontend needs can be fulfilled by this single server from a classical web development perspective. It is served through \textbf{Render} with future scalability in mind. This hosts the merchant flows we learned about before and the dashboard as well.
  \item \textbf{Integration Layer - Backend}: The carrier integration layer is built into many of the backend endpoints, and they mainly cover the integration management and shipment management flows described earlier. These are imported as regular \textbf{npm} packages and directly used to communicate with the carriers' APIs without any additional API gateway or broker, as each merchant is using their own instance and credentials, there are no shared resources or rate limits to worry about generally. (The exception to this is event handling and tracking services that require more control.)
  \item \textbf{Database and Storage}: The database was chosen to be as open as possible to avoid vendor lock-in and to allow for future flexibility via extensions. The choice ended on \textbf{PostgreSQL} hosted through \textbf{Supabase} and managed through \textbf{Prisma} as the ORM. This choice allows for a good balance between ease of use, scalability, and extensibility, as well as being a common choice in the industry. Another benefit of \textbf{Supabase} is the integrated and generous S3-compatible storage buckets, which are used for storing label PDFs and other documents related to shipments.
  \item \textbf{Webhook Handlers}: \textit{Shopify} provides ways to register to events happening in the merchant's store, such as new orders and order updates. These are utilized to synchronize state to our database and automatically take actions on the merchant's behalf. This requires resilience and retry mechanisms, for which we use \textbf{Inngest} as an event-driven workflow orchestration and durable execution platform. It has the ability to queue and retry failed jobs, without adding the overhead and complexity of additional infrastructure, as it runs our preexisting \textit{Node.js} server.
  \item \textbf{Cron Jobs}: There are also multiple cron jobs to consider, such as the ones used for tracking active shipment statuses, or the ones calculating and updating dashboard's KPIs. These are also implemented using \textbf{Inngest} and run on the same server as the backend, but they are scheduled to run at specific intervals, such as every 5 minutes or every hour, depending on the use case. With shipment tracking for example, since we are once again making use of the merchant's API key access and not ours, we do not need to have serious gateway rules set aside from a simple exponential backoff and retry mechanism, while making sure to respect carrier's guidance with our scheduling. (e.g., in case of MPL, they recommend to only poll a shipment every four hours.)
  \item \textbf{Integration Layer - Cron}: The integration layer is also used the cron jobs for tracking each merchant's shipments and updating their status in the database, as well as updating pickup points and other relevant data from the carriers. This is done through the same \textbf{npm} packages as the backend, but in a different context. Since these jobs are either on a larger system level scale, or even in case of per-merchant scale could run into the hundreds of requests per hour, we have to be careful with scheduling and queuing.
  \item \textbf{Shopify Theme Extension - App Block}: The pickup point picker widget is a completely separate piece of frontend code that needs to build to \textit{WebAssembly} as per Shopify's requirements. It was built using \textbf{SolidJS} and serves as an interface for customers to select their preferred delivery options during checkout. It needs to both communicate with our backend server and Shopify's APIs to function properly, and it is designed to be as lightweight and fast as possible to not overburden merchants' storefronts.
  \item \textbf{Error Tracking and Telemetry}: For all of the above component, including the backend, frontend and the Shopify app block, we use \textbf{Sentry} for error tracking and telemetry. This allows us to monitor the health of the application, catch errors in real-time, and gain insights into user behavior and performance metrics. It is crucial for maintaining a high-quality user experience and quickly addressing any issues that arise.
\end{itemize}

One of the main challenges is interactions with external systems, such as the shipping providers, because we have no control over their APIs, nor have any idea of their inner workings.
Being able to communicate with multiple providers using a unified and consistent interface could save a lot of time and effort for developers and allow them to focus on the business logic, rather than the brittle, custom one-to-one function mappings that are usually built on a per application basis.
In this case, overall development time was not shortened, as not only the provider adapters, but the core also needed to be built from scratch, but once it was time to implement the capabilities in the app, it was a much better developer experience.
Conclusion of \textbf{MRQ1} from a practical perspective is that while it is not necessarily faster, the adapter + API gateway architecture is a suitable trade-off for extensibility and operational effort, as it allows for a unified interface to multiple providers, while still allowing for provider-specific customizations and extensions and is seemingly a better user experience.

\section{Dashboard Implementation}
The dashboard is a central component of the \textit{Shopickup} application and serves as the topic for our second set of main and sub-research questions.
It is designed to provide merchants with a comprehensive overview of their shipping performance, including key performance indicators (KPIs), metrics, and alerts related to their shipments and carriers.

\subsubsection{Real-Time Alert and Monitoring System}
The alert feed described in the second tier of the layout is backed by a server-side alerting pipeline.
Four categories of alerts are generated automatically by the system based on tracking events and the incoming webhook events.

\begin{enumerate}[left=0pt, itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{SLA Breaches}: When a tracking-event webhook or periodic evaluation detects that a shipment is in transit but past its promised delivery date, a \texttt{warning} alert is created. Shipments approaching their deadline (within 24 hours) generate a \texttt{warning} \textit{SLA at-risk} alert instead. (Operational, Table \ref{tab:operational-kpis} row 1)
  \item \textbf{1-Day Carrier SLA Breaches}: For shipments tagged with \texttt{oneDaySlaActive} (carrier-guaranteed 24-hour delivery from handover), a \texttt{critical} alert is raised when 24 hours elapse without a delivery event. (Operational, Table \ref{tab:operational-kpis} row 2)
  \item \textbf{Damage and Exception Detection}: When a tracking event arrives with a status that indicates an exception or damage, the shipment is automatically flagged in the database accordingly and a \texttt{warning} level alert notifies the merchant. (Strategic, Table \ref{tab:strategic-kpis} row 4)
  \item \textbf{Order and Invoice Validation}: When a new order is created, the system does preliminary validation of the order details and flags inconsistencies both relevant for shipping (e.g., missing address or recipient details) and for accounting (e.g., nonsensical VAT numbers). A \texttt{warning} alert is raised for any detected issues. (Operational, Table \ref{tab:operational-kpis} row 4)
\end{enumerate}

In addition to these high-priority alerts, the system also generates \texttt{info} alerts for non-critical events, such as new features, upcoming maintenance, and other informational messages, which are manually issued by the system administrators.
All alerts are deduplicated within a 24-hour window per (shop, category, related entity), pinned critical alerts appear permanently expanded at the top of the feed, and every alert carries a server-side dismiss action guarded by UUID validation.
In demo mode, four realistic sample alerts (SLA breach, delayed delivery, flagged order requiring review, and a new-feature announcement) are presented with a \texttt{[DEMO]} prefix to avoid confusing merchants.

Tying these findings back to the research questions, we can now state explicit conclusions for the second half of this study.

Conclusion of \textbf{MRQ2}: the hybrid Speed/Batch/Serving Layer design (Section \ref{sec:dashboard-design}), inspired by the Lambda Architecture, proved to be an appropriate way to balance usability, scalability, and real-time needs. Both merchants relied on the Speed Layer's real-time SLA and breach alerts for day-to-day operational decisions, while independently using the Batch Layer's strategic visualizations (cost comparisons, delivery-time trends) for slower, planning-oriented decisions. This confirms that a single dashboard can serve both operational and strategic decision horizons without merchants needing to distinguish between the underlying data layers themselves, satisfying the usability requirement of MRQ2.

Conclusion of \textbf{SRQ2.1}: across both interviews, the most consistently valued KPIs were OTDR (and its SLA-breach variant), per-carrier delivery/cycle times, and carrier cost comparisons; these were the metrics merchants said they checked regularly and used to make concrete decisions (e.g., adjusting shipping method pricing, timing seasonal shipments). Conversely, metrics such as damage rate and the single-value Perfect Order Rate were considered less critical by at least one merchant, suggesting that KPI relevance for SMEs is not uniform and depends on business model (e.g., made-to-order vs. stock-based). The interviews also surfaced a KPI gap not covered by the literature reviewed in Chapter \ref{ch:related}: metrics around Cash-on-Delivery (COD) and pickup-point non-collection, which both merchants independently flagged as their biggest operational pain point.


\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

This chapter interprets the results of the evaluation, discusses the trade-offs made during the architectural design, and addresses the limitations of the current prototype.

\section{Interpretation of Findings}
The qualitative interviews provided valuable insights into the practical challenges SMEs face regarding shipping operations and how a unified platform addresses them.
Overall, the findings confirm that the dashboard's KPI-driven approach significantly improves operational visibility compared to fragmented, manual tracking workflows.

A prominent theme that emerged from both merchants was the unexpectedly high impact of COD and pickup point orders.
Merchants highlighted that uncollected packages at automated parcel machines result not only in lost sales but also in wasted material and shipping costs.
This finding suggests a gap in the broader logistics literature, which tends to focus on global supply chain metrics rather than the localized challenges of APM adoption and payment methods common in the Hungarian market.

The merchants also validated the dashboard's layered design.
The Speed Layer's real-time alerts for SLA breaches and operational anomalies were cited as core differentiators, allowing merchants to preemptively address issues.
Meanwhile, the Batch Layer's strategic visualizations—such as carrier cost comparisons and delivery cycle times—were recognized as valuable tools for long-term planning and contract evaluation.
The feedback indicates that while automated and single-metric approaches are highly valued, merchants still desire granular control, such as customizable alert thresholds and export functionality for offline review.

\section{Limitations and Scope}
It is important to address some of the shortcomings of our work and the research done, as well as the limitations of the system and its practical application.

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Qualitative research}: The main shortcoming compared to the original plan was the evaluation of the dashboard.
        It was supposed to be carried out in a quantitative manner using surveys and analytics, which could have given us a more objective and statistically significant result, but due to \textit{Shopify's} rigorous app review process that may require more than two-three months at this time for newcomers.
        This is an unfortunate and unpredictable factor in our planning, as this heightened waiting time is a temporary issue on \textit{Shopify's} side, but it is not something we can control or predict, and it has a direct impact on our ability to conduct the evaluation in a timely manner.
        Because of not having passed review yet, the application is not yet available to the public through \textit{Shopify's} app store, and thus we cannot reach a wide enough audience to conduct the evaluation as planned.
        Instead, we opted for a workaround with the qualitative research described in section \ref{sec:qualitative-interviews} via custom per customer app deployments.
  \item \textbf{Platform agnosticism}: While the backend is built in a way where most of the logic is platform-agnostic and reusable, at the time of writing the frontend is primarily designed to integrate with \textit{Shopify's} admin dashboard via an embedded app. As mentioned before, this is a constraint imposed by the platform as the applications have to adhere to certain rules to be servable through \textit{iframes} in their admin UI.
  \item \textbf{International carriers}: Although the carrier integration system verifiably works with multiple Hungarian carriers (\textit{GLS}, \textit{MPL}, \textit{Foxpost}), it has not yet been tested with non-domestic carriers, such as \textit{Austrian Post}. While international carrier support was not named directly in the scope of the thesis, it would have been a natural extension of the work done, further proving the system's flexibility and extensibility.
\end{itemize}


\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}
The primary objective of this thesis was to design, develop, and evaluate a scalable software architecture that unifies e-commerce shipping operations.
Through the creation of the \textit{Shopickup} prototype, an extensible API adapter layer was realized that successfully abstracts the heterogeneity of various carrier APIs.

By applying SOA principles and utilizing an Adapter and API Gateway pattern, the system demonstrated that it is possible to decouple core e-commerce logic from carrier-specific implementations.
This architectural approach not only simplifies the integration of new carriers but also ensures maintainability and operational resilience.

Furthermore, the implementation of a KPI-driven analytics dashboard was carried out using a hybrid Speed and Batch Layer architecture that proved effective in translating raw shipping data into actionable business intelligence.
Qualitative evaluations confirmed that merchants derive significant value from real-time operational alerts and strategic performance visualizations, effectively mitigating the inefficiencies of fragmented shipping workflows.

\section{Future Work}
Future development of the \textit{Shopickup} platform and further research in this domain could involve:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Global Carrier Expansion}: Full integration with a wider array of global and regional carriers to thoroughly test the limits of the adapter abstraction and ensure the core canonical models are universally applicable.
  \item \textbf{Open-Source Community Engagement}: Leveraging the open-source nature of the project to foster a community of contributors, which could accelerate the addition of new carriers and features while also providing real-world feedback on system performance and usability.
  \item \textbf{Quantitative Evaluation}: Conducting a larger-scale, quantitative evaluation of the dashboard's impact on operational efficiency and decision-making, potentially through A/B testing or controlled trials with multiple merchants.
  \item \textbf{COD and Pickup Point Analytics}: Conducting a focused study on the challenges and best practices surrounding COD and pickup point orders to address the KPI gap identified in the qualitative interviews, potentially leading to new features or metrics that better support merchants in these areas.
  \item \textbf{Enhanced Notification Channels}: Expanding the alert system to include configurable external channels, such as automated email forwarding or SMS notifications, particularly targeting the management of uncollected COD and pickup point orders.
  \item \textbf{Predictive Analytics}: Implementing more complex, event-driven analytics utilizing machine learning models for predictive shipping delays and automated carrier routing based on historical performance.
\end{itemize}
